5 Botox Myths

Our physicians hear these questions every week — here's what's actually true.

Injectables · 6 Min Read · By Dr. Elaine Chen, MD

Few aesthetic treatments are as misunderstood as Botox. Despite decades of FDA approval and millions of treatments performed safely each year, the myths persist. Here are the five we hear most often at Lumina — and the clinical reality behind each.

Myth 1: "Botox will freeze my face."

This fear usually comes from seeing over-treated results, not properly dosed ones. In skilled hands, Botox relaxes only the targeted muscles responsible for specific dynamic lines — your ability to smile, raise your brows, and express emotion remains fully intact. The "frozen" look is almost always a dosing or placement error, not an inherent effect of the product.

Myth 2: "It's only for older patients."

Many of our patients start preventive Botox in their late 20s and early 30s — treating dynamic lines before they become etched into the skin at rest is one of the most effective long-term strategies in aesthetic medicine.

Myth 3: "The injections are extremely painful."

Most patients describe the sensation as a brief pinch, comparable to a minor pinprick. Treatment typically takes 15–20 minutes, and numbing cream is available for patients with lower pain tolerance.

Myth 4: "Once you start, you can never stop."

Botox is not habit-forming or physiologically necessary. If you stop treatment, your muscles simply return to their natural movement pattern over the following weeks — there is no rebound effect or dependency.

Myth 5: "All Botox is the same."

Formulation, dosing, and — most importantly — the skill of your injector all affect outcome significantly. This is why Lumina only uses board-certified physicians and extensively trained nurse injectors for every treatment, never undertrained staff.

If you're curious whether Botox is right for you, our physicians are happy to walk through your specific concerns at a complimentary consultation — no pressure, no commission-based upselling.
